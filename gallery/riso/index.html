How it’s made

The look borrows from real risograph printing — studied from a designer’s breakdown of the medium, not a code tutorial: three ink plates (forest, teal, red) screened into 45°-family halftone dots over warm paper, each plate printed slightly off-register. The renderer is hand-written Canvas2D — tone maps to dot radius per plate, and multiply compositing builds the third colours where inks overlap.

The tonal core is a pure, DOM-free module with unit tests; the canvas layer only samples the photo and draws. It also only redraws while the pointer moves — there is no always-on animation loop.

The source photo ships as a 135KB indexed PNG, baked offline from a 969KB original. Because the piece samples it coarsely through the dot grid, the optimisation was verified not with a pixel diff but by re-running the halftone pipeline on both assets and comparing the dots it produces: mean radius difference 0.31px — invisible at screen scale.
